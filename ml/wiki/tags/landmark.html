  • KL DivergenceAn information-theoretic measure of how one probability distribution differs from a reference distribution — the asymmetric "extra bits" needed to encode samples from \( p \) using a code optimized for \( q \). It underpins maximum-likelihood estimation, variational inference, RLHF, and countless other learning objectives.
  • Jensen's InequalityFor any convex function \( \varphi \) and random variable \( X \), \( \varphi(\mathbb{E}[X]) \le \mathbb{E}[\varphi(X)] \). This single inequality is the engine behind the non-negativity of KL divergence, the ELBO, the concavity of entropy, and most convergence proofs in machine learning.
  • Bayes' TheoremA rule for updating beliefs in light of new evidence: the posterior probability of a hypothesis given data is proportional to the likelihood of the data times the prior. It is the foundation of probabilistic inference and distinguishes Bayesian from frequentist statistics.
  • Universal Approximation TheoremA family of results showing that feed-forward neural networks with a single hidden layer and a suitable non-polynomial activation can approximate any continuous function on a compact set to arbitrary accuracy, given enough hidden units. The theorem is an existence guarantee — it says nothing about training, sample complexity, or how wide the network must be.
  • Double DescentThe empirical phenomenon where test error, as a function of model complexity, first follows the classical U-shape (under-fit → sweet spot → over-fit), then <i>descends again</i> once the model is large enough to interpolate the training data. Observed in random features, kernel methods, and deep networks, double descent is the clearest visual contradiction to the textbook bias–variance story.
  • Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK)In the limit of infinite width and small learning rate, a fully-trained neural network behaves like kernel regression with a deterministic kernel — the Neural Tangent Kernel — that depends only on the architecture, not on the particular initialisation. The NTK gave the first rigorous connection between deep networks and reproducing-kernel Hilbert spaces and is a major theoretical tool for analysing optimisation and generalisation.
  • Chinchilla Scaling LawsDeepMind's 2022 analysis (Hoffmann et al.) showing that, under a fixed compute budget \( C \approx 6 N D \), optimal pretraining allocates <b>roughly equal compute to parameters and data</b> — meaning parameters \( N \) and training tokens \( D \) should scale in lockstep. This corrected the earlier Kaplan et al. (2020) recommendation to over-grow models and led to the "Chinchilla-optimal" rule-of-thumb \( D \approx 20 N \).
  • REINFORCE AlgorithmThe classical policy-gradient algorithm (Williams, 1992): estimate \( \nabla_\theta J(\theta) \) by Monte-Carlo-sampling trajectories and weighting the log-probability of each action by the (possibly baseline-subtracted) return. All modern policy-gradient methods — A2C, PPO, GRPO, RLHF — are descendants of this one estimator.
  • Actor–Critic MethodsA hybrid policy-gradient framework that learns both a policy ("actor") and a value function ("critic") simultaneously. The critic's estimates serve as a learned baseline that sharply reduces the variance of the policy gradient, enabling stable on-policy RL at scale.
  • Reasoning Models (o1 / R1-style Long-CoT)A class of LLMs that spend disproportionate <i>inference</i> compute on internal chain-of-thought before answering. OpenAI's o1 and DeepSeek-R1 showed that pure-RL training on verifiable reward (math, code) elicits emergent behaviours — backtracking, self-correction, hypothesis enumeration — and that test-time compute scales monotonically with accuracy.
  • Vision Transformer (ViT)Dosovitskiy et al. (2020) showed that a pure Transformer applied to fixed-size image patches as tokens matches or exceeds state-of-the-art CNNs on ImageNet when pretrained on enough data. ViT is the backbone of modern vision-language models (CLIP, SigLIP, DINOv2, MAE) and the foundation of nearly all 2020s visual representation work.
  • Masked Autoencoder (MAE)A self-supervised ViT pretraining objective: randomly mask 75% of image patches and train an asymmetric encoder–decoder to reconstruct pixel values from the visible 25%. MAE is simple, compute-efficient (the encoder sees only unmasked patches), and produces state-of-the-art ImageNet fine-tuning representations.
  • Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM)A generative model that learns to reverse a fixed Gaussian corruption process. Ho et al. (2020) showed that predicting the added noise with a neural network, trained by a simple MSE loss on \( T \) diffusion steps, yields state-of-the-art image synthesis — the foundation of all modern image/video diffusion.
  • Latent DiffusionRun the diffusion process in the compressed latent space of a pretrained VAE rather than in pixel space. Latent diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) slashes memory and compute by ~8× for images while preserving sample quality, and is the architecture behind Stable Diffusion, SDXL, SD3, and most text-to-image systems.

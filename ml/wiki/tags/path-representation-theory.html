Tag: path-representation-theory

10 topic(s)

  • Contrastive Learning TheoryThe theoretical account of why contrastive self-supervised objectives like InfoNCE produce useful representations. Wang & Isola (2020) show the InfoNCE loss decomposes into two asymptotic terms — \( \mathcal{L}_{\text{align}} \), pulling positive pairs together, and \( \mathcal{L}_{\text{unif}} \), spreading the marginal feature distribution uniformly on the hypersphere. The downstream linear-probe accuracy correlates almost perfectly with this alignment-uniformity trade-off, giving a geometric explanation for why contrastive learning works at all.
  • Non-Contrastive SSL (BYOL, SimSiam, Barlow Twins, VICReg)Self-supervised methods that learn from positive pairs alone — no negatives — and yet do not collapse to a constant. BYOL (Grill et al. 2020) and SimSiam (Chen & He 2021) achieve this by an asymmetric architecture: a predictor MLP on one branch and a stop-gradient on the other. Barlow Twins (Zbontar et al. 2021) and VICReg (Bardes et al. 2022) instead enforce non-collapse explicitly via a feature covariance regulariser. The common theme is replacing the contrastive uniformity term by an architectural or statistical anti-collapse mechanism.
  • Disentangled Representation LearningThe pursuit of latent variables \( z = (z_1, \ldots, z_d) \) whose components correspond to independent semantic factors of variation (pose, identity, lighting). β-VAE (Higgins et al. 2017) up-weights the KL term in the VAE ELBO to encourage axis-aligned independent posteriors; FactorVAE (Kim & Mnih 2018) penalises the total correlation directly. Locatello et al. (2019) prove that fully unsupervised disentanglement is impossible without inductive biases, reframing the field around weak supervision and benchmark-driven evaluation.
  • Sparse Representations in Deep NetsModern deep networks naturally produce sparse activations: ReLU networks have median activation sparsity \( \sim 50\% \); transformer MLPs reach \( 80–95\% \) per-token sparsity in late layers. Sparsity is a free regulariser, an exploitable inference acceleration target, and a theoretical lens — the lottery-ticket hypothesis (Frankle & Carbin 2019) shows dense networks contain sparse subnetworks that train to full accuracy in isolation. Recent work (Mirzadeh et al. 2024, Liu et al. 2023) leverages activation sparsity for 2–10× LLM inference speedups via top-k routing.
  • Representation CollapseA failure mode of representation learning where the encoder produces low-rank or constant features, eliminating useful signal. Three common modes: <b>complete collapse</b> (\( f \equiv c \), seen in non-contrastive SSL without anti-collapse mechanisms), <b>dimensional collapse</b> (Jing et al. 2022 — features span a low-dimensional subspace), and <b>neural collapse</b> (Papyan, Han & Donoho 2020 — late-training features cluster onto class-mean simplex). Each mode has distinct diagnostics (feature variance, eigenspectrum decay, simplex angle) and distinct mitigations.
  • Invariance vs EquivarianceA representation \( f: X \to Z \) is <i>invariant</i> to a group action \( g \in G \) if \( f(g \cdot x) = f(x) \), and <i>equivariant</i> if \( f(g \cdot x) = \rho_Z(g) \cdot f(x) \) for some representation \( \rho_Z \) of \( G \) on \( Z \). Invariance throws away the group's information; equivariance preserves it in a structured form. Choosing one over the other depends on whether the downstream task needs to recover the group element. CNNs are translation-equivariant; group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling 2016) extend this to rotations, reflections, and beyond.
  • Embedding GeometryThe geometry of learned vector representations — how distance, angle, and clustering structure encode semantics. Word2vec produced the famous parallelogram \( \vec{\text{king}} - \vec{\text{man}} + \vec{\text{woman}} \approx \vec{\text{queen}} \) (Mikolov et al. 2013). Modern embeddings exhibit anisotropy — token embeddings cluster in narrow cones — addressed by whitening and isotropy-promoting losses. Cosine similarity dominates as the standard metric because magnitude in trained embeddings often confounds frequency rather than semantics.
  • Metric Learning at ScaleMetric learning trains an encoder so that distances in embedding space match a semantic similarity. At billion-vector scale (web search, face identification, ad recommendation), the bottleneck shifts from loss design to nearest-neighbour data structures (HNSW, IVF-PQ in FAISS), product quantization for index compression, and hard-negative mining strategies that don't blow up training cost. ArcFace's angular margin loss (Deng et al. 2019) and proxy losses replaced triplet/contrastive once the negatives became impractical to enumerate.
  • Representation Alignment Across ModalitiesCross-modal alignment trains separate encoders for different input types (image, text, audio) so that paired examples land near each other in a shared embedding space. CLIP (Radford et al. 2021) uses a symmetric InfoNCE on \( 4 \times 10^8 \) image–text pairs from the web, learning a joint space where zero-shot image classification is performed by similarity to a text prototype. The geometry of the joint space is the entire interface between modalities — downstream tasks (retrieval, classification, generation guidance) operate on this aligned embedding rather than on raw pixels or tokens.
  • Tokenization as RepresentationTokenization is a representation choice, not a preprocessing detail — it determines what the model can and cannot see. BPE/Unigram merges encode frequency-weighted statistical priors over the corpus; the vocabulary fixes the model's lexical resolution; and morphologically poor segmentations (digit splits, code-token splits, multilingual under-coverage) cap downstream performance regardless of model size. Recent work (Limisiewicz et al. 2024, Tay et al. 2022) treats the tokenizer as a learnable component co-designed with the model.

  • Transparency (AI Systems)The principle that AI systems should provide clear, accessible documentation regarding their architecture, training data, inherent capabilities, and failure modes to enable structured accountability and human oversight.
  • RLAIF (RL from AI Feedback)Replace the human annotator in RLHF with a strong LLM that generates preference labels from a rubric. RLAIF scales preference collection by orders of magnitude, achieves parity with RLHF on helpfulness/harmlessness, and is the dominant pipeline for safety and reasoning alignment at modern-frontier scale.
  • Reasoning Models (o1 / R1-style Long-CoT)A class of LLMs that spend disproportionate <i>inference</i> compute on internal chain-of-thought before answering. OpenAI's o1 and DeepSeek-R1 showed that pure-RL training on verifiable reward (math, code) elicits emergent behaviours — backtracking, self-correction, hypothesis enumeration — and that test-time compute scales monotonically with accuracy.
  • vLLM & Continuous BatchingvLLM is an open-source LLM serving system built around PagedAttention and <i>continuous batching</i>: a scheduler admits, evicts, and interleaves requests at every decoding iteration, eliminating the padding and head-of-line blocking that plague static batching. vLLM routinely delivers 5–20× throughput over naive HuggingFace serving at the same latency.
  • Core LLM Benchmarks (MMLU, HumanEval, GSM8K, MATH)Four widely reported LLM evaluation suites. MMLU: 57-subject multiple-choice knowledge. HumanEval: Python function synthesis from docstrings, graded by unit tests. GSM8K: ~8.5K grade-school multi-step word problems. MATH: competition math with written-derivation answers. Each stresses a different capability and has well-known failure modes.
  • LMSYS Chatbot ArenaA crowdsourced pairwise-preference leaderboard where anonymous users chat with two LLMs in parallel and vote for the better response. Bradley–Terry-fit Elo ratings produce a continuously-updated ranking that is arguably the most informative public signal of frontier-model quality outside automated benchmarks.
  • Stable Diffusion PipelineA text-to-image pipeline composed of (i) a VAE that compresses pixels to a 64×-smaller latent, (ii) a text encoder (CLIP) that provides conditioning, and (iii) a diffusion U-Net (or DiT) that denoises in latent space. All three pretrained components are glued by classifier-free guidance at inference.
  • Whisper (Speech-to-Text)OpenAI's 2022 encoder-decoder Transformer trained on 680k hours of weakly supervised multilingual audio-text pairs. Whisper performs speech recognition, translation, and voice-activity / language ID from a single model, with strong zero-shot robustness to noise, accent, and domain shift.
  • KV Cache Compression (H2O, SnapKV)Inference-time methods that shrink a long-context KV cache by evicting tokens that contribute little to future attention. H2O (Zhang et al., 2023) evicts by cumulative attention score; SnapKV (Li et al., 2024) observes that recent queries already reveal which past tokens matter, enabling one-shot pre-fill-time compression.
  • Chunked PrefillA serving-time technique that breaks the long prefill of a prompt into small chunks and interleaves them with decode steps of other requests. By keeping GPU utilisation high during prefill and avoiding long tail latencies, chunked prefill dramatically improves throughput in mixed-batch LLM serving.
  • Disaggregated Prefill/Decode ServingAn LLM-serving architecture that runs prefill and decode on <b>separate</b> GPU pools, transferring the KV cache from prefill nodes to decode nodes. Eliminates resource contention between the two phases; adopted by DistServe, Splitwise, Mooncake for frontier-scale serving.
  • Paged vs Block KV CacheTwo allocation strategies for an LLM's growing KV cache. <b>Block</b> (contiguous) allocation pre-reserves the worst-case length per request and wastes memory. <b>Paged</b> (PagedAttention, vLLM 2023) allocates fixed-size pages on demand and chains them like OS virtual memory, yielding 2–4× higher batch-size at the cost of kernel-level bookkeeping.
  • Best-of-N Sampling and its ScalingAn inference-time boost: sample \( N \) responses from a language model, score them with a reward model or verifier, and return the best. Quality scales with \( \log N \); scaling laws predict the knob where best-of-\( N \) inference compute equals extra training compute, and motivate distillation of best-of-\( N \) behaviour back into the policy via RFT.
  • Weak-to-Strong GeneralizationOpenAI's analog for scalable oversight (Burns et al., 2023): can a strong model, fine-tuned on labels from a weaker supervisor, generalise beyond the supervisor's capability? Experiments on NLP and chess tasks show it partially can; the residual quality gap motivates future work on supervising superhuman models.
  • Tool Use / Function Calling Benchmarks (BFCL, τ-bench)<b>BFCL</b> (Berkeley Function Calling Leaderboard, 2024) and <b>τ-bench</b> (2024) evaluate LLMs' ability to select, parameterise, and sequence API calls. BFCL is single-turn function-call accuracy; τ-bench is multi-turn dialogue in simulated customer-service environments with realistic state and policy constraints.
  • Agent Benchmarks (SWE-bench, GAIA, WebArena)<b>SWE-bench</b> (Jimenez et al., 2023) — resolve real GitHub issues. <b>GAIA</b> (Mialon et al., 2023) — general-assistant questions requiring web search, reasoning, and tool use. <b>WebArena</b> (Zhou et al., 2023) — navigate realistic simulated websites to complete multi-step tasks. Together they are the 2024–2026 frontier-model yardstick for agent capability.

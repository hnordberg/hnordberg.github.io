  • Double DescentThe empirical phenomenon where test error, as a function of model complexity, first follows the classical U-shape (under-fit → sweet spot → over-fit), then <i>descends again</i> once the model is large enough to interpolate the training data. Observed in random features, kernel methods, and deep networks, double descent is the clearest visual contradiction to the textbook bias–variance story.
  • GrokkingA late-generalisation phenomenon where a network first memorises the training set (near-perfect train, near-chance test) and then, after many additional steps of training, suddenly generalises — test accuracy shooting from chance to near-perfect. Discovered on small arithmetic tasks, grokking reveals that generalisation and memorisation can be decoupled in time and that weight decay / optimisation dynamics play the central role.
  • Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK)In the limit of infinite width and small learning rate, a fully-trained neural network behaves like kernel regression with a deterministic kernel — the Neural Tangent Kernel — that depends only on the architecture, not on the particular initialisation. The NTK gave the first rigorous connection between deep networks and reproducing-kernel Hilbert spaces and is a major theoretical tool for analysing optimisation and generalisation.
  • State Space Models / MambaA family of sequence models that evolve a hidden state through a linear recurrence \( h_t = \bar{A} h_{t-1} + \bar{B} x_t \), \( y_t = C h_t \), making them RNN-like at inference yet convolutional and parallel at training. Mamba (Gu &amp; Dao, 2023) added input-dependent ("selective") transitions and an efficient hardware-aware scan, turning SSMs into competitive Transformer alternatives with linear complexity in sequence length.
  • Linear AttentionA family of attention mechanisms that replace the softmax similarity in standard attention with a kernel feature map, collapsing the \( O(T^2) \) complexity of attention into \( O(T) \). Although lossy compared to softmax attention, linear attention is central to efficient Transformers (Performer, Linear Transformer) and deeply related to modern State Space Models.
  • ALiBi (Attention with Linear Biases)A positional encoding for Transformers that adds a static, head-specific linear penalty \( -m_h \cdot (i - j) \) directly into the attention logits, replacing sinusoidal or learned positional embeddings. Its training-free length extrapolation property — models trained on 1K tokens evaluate well on much longer sequences — made ALiBi influential before RoPE + NTK-style scaling took over.
  • YaRN / NTK-aware RoPE ScalingA family of techniques for extending the usable context length of a RoPE-based Transformer beyond its training length by <i>re-parameterising</i> the rotary frequencies rather than retraining from scratch. Position Interpolation, NTK-aware scaling, and YaRN (Peng et al., 2023) each preserve high-frequency components while compressing low-frequency ones, giving massive context extension (e.g., 4K → 128K) with only a short fine-tune.
  • Sliding-Window AttentionAn attention pattern that restricts each query to attend only within a local window of the last \( w \) tokens, reducing the cost per query from \( O(T) \) to \( O(w) \) and the KV cache from \( O(T) \) to \( O(w) \) per layer. Combined with global tokens (Longformer), dilated windows (BigBird), or a few full-attention layers (Mistral), it is a ubiquitous tool for scaling Transformers to long contexts without abandoning softmax attention entirely.
  • Multi-head Latent Attention (MLA)An attention variant introduced in DeepSeek-V2 (2024) that compresses the keys and values of multi-head attention through a low-rank <i>latent</i> projection, shrinking the KV cache by an order of magnitude while maintaining or improving quality over grouped-query attention. MLA is the dominant KV-cache compression technique in the DeepSeek / Qwen-2.5 / open-weight MoE family.
  • Chinchilla Scaling LawsDeepMind's 2022 analysis (Hoffmann et al.) showing that, under a fixed compute budget \( C \approx 6 N D \), optimal pretraining allocates <b>roughly equal compute to parameters and data</b> — meaning parameters \( N \) and training tokens \( D \) should scale in lockstep. This corrected the earlier Kaplan et al. (2020) recommendation to over-grow models and led to the "Chinchilla-optimal" rule-of-thumb \( D \approx 20 N \).
  • Auxiliary Load-Balancing Loss (MoE)A differentiable penalty added to a sparse Mixture-of-Experts training objective that pushes the router toward an even token distribution across experts, preventing the positive-feedback collapse where most tokens are sent to a few dominant experts.
  • Muon OptimizerA second-order-style optimizer for hidden matrix weights that replaces each parameter-space gradient with its orthogonalized version (via a few Newton–Schulz iterations), effectively performing steepest descent under the spectral norm. Muon trains transformers measurably faster than AdamW on matching compute budgets.
  • Generalized Advantage Estimation (GAE)A family of advantage estimators that interpolates between low-variance 1-step TD residuals and high-variance Monte-Carlo returns, indexed by a parameter \( \lambda \in [0, 1] \). GAE is the default advantage signal in PPO and most modern on-policy RL.
  • Process Reward Models (PRM) vs Outcome Reward Models (ORM)Two ways to score a multi-step solution: an ORM gives a single scalar for the final answer; a PRM grades each intermediate step. PRMs provide denser, per-step signal — essential for training and search in reasoning models, but expensive to annotate and vulnerable to reward hacking.
  • KTO (Kahneman–Tversky Optimization)A preference-optimization objective that trains on single-sided labels ("this completion is good" / "this completion is bad") instead of pairwise comparisons. KTO uses a reference-model-relative utility inspired by Kahneman–Tversky prospect theory: loss-aversion asymmetrically penalises completions that fall below the reference-implicit utility threshold.
  • RLAIF (RL from AI Feedback)Replace the human annotator in RLHF with a strong LLM that generates preference labels from a rubric. RLAIF scales preference collection by orders of magnitude, achieves parity with RLHF on helpfulness/harmlessness, and is the dominant pipeline for safety and reasoning alignment at modern-frontier scale.
  • Reasoning Models (o1 / R1-style Long-CoT)A class of LLMs that spend disproportionate <i>inference</i> compute on internal chain-of-thought before answering. OpenAI's o1 and DeepSeek-R1 showed that pure-RL training on verifiable reward (math, code) elicits emergent behaviours — backtracking, self-correction, hypothesis enumeration — and that test-time compute scales monotonically with accuracy.
  • Process SupervisionTraining a model (usually a process reward model) on step-level correctness labels rather than only on final-answer correctness. Process supervision gives denser credit assignment, makes verification tractable for long reasoning chains, and underpins the best published results on MATH.
  • Monte Carlo Tree Search for LLM ReasoningApply the AlphaZero-style MCTS framework to tree-structured LLM reasoning: expand candidate continuations as children of the current partial solution, evaluate leaves with a value/PRM, and select promising branches via UCB. MCTS is the default search procedure for deliberate test-time reasoning when a step-level scorer is available.
  • vLLM & Continuous BatchingvLLM is an open-source LLM serving system built around PagedAttention and <i>continuous batching</i>: a scheduler admits, evicts, and interleaves requests at every decoding iteration, eliminating the padding and head-of-line blocking that plague static batching. vLLM routinely delivers 5–20× throughput over naive HuggingFace serving at the same latency.
  • GPTQ QuantizationA one-shot, layer-wise post-training quantization that pushes LLM weights to 3–4 bits while preserving generation quality. GPTQ reformulates quantization as a per-row error-minimisation problem solved greedily using the inverse Hessian of a small calibration set, achieving 3-bit LLaMA-65B with <1% perplexity loss.
  • AWQ (Activation-aware Weight Quantization)A post-training quantization that protects the ~1% of weights connected to the largest-magnitude activation channels by scaling them before rounding. AWQ achieves 4-bit LLM inference with negligible quality loss, is ~simpler than GPTQ, and is widely deployed via kernels in TensorRT-LLM and vLLM.
  • Attention Sinks / StreamingLLMAn empirical phenomenon in causal Transformers: the first few tokens receive disproportionately large attention weight from <i>all</i> later queries, even when they carry no semantic value. Keeping these tokens pinned in the KV-cache (StreamingLLM) lets pretrained models generate unbounded streams without quality collapse.
  • Induction HeadsA specific two-head circuit in Transformer attention that copies the next token after a previous occurrence of the current token — the computational basis for in-context learning. Anthropic showed induction heads form suddenly during training, coinciding with the sharp jump in ICL ability.
  • Circuit AnalysisThe mechanistic-interpretability practice of identifying subgraphs of weights, residual-stream components, and attention heads that jointly implement a human-interpretable algorithm (indirect object identification, modular addition, greater-than). Circuit analysis produces falsifiable, causal accounts of what a network has learned.
  • ROME / MEMIT Model EditingRank-one edits to MLP weights that inject a single fact (ROME) or thousands of facts (MEMIT) into a pretrained LLM without retraining. They exploit the observation that MLP blocks act as key–value memories, identify the causal neurons via activation patching, and solve a closed-form optimisation problem for the minimal-norm weight update.
  • Vision Transformer (ViT)Dosovitskiy et al. (2020) showed that a pure Transformer applied to fixed-size image patches as tokens matches or exceeds state-of-the-art CNNs on ImageNet when pretrained on enough data. ViT is the backbone of modern vision-language models (CLIP, SigLIP, DINOv2, MAE) and the foundation of nearly all 2020s visual representation work.
  • Masked Autoencoder (MAE)A self-supervised ViT pretraining objective: randomly mask 75% of image patches and train an asymmetric encoder–decoder to reconstruct pixel values from the visible 25%. MAE is simple, compute-efficient (the encoder sees only unmasked patches), and produces state-of-the-art ImageNet fine-tuning representations.
  • DINOv2A self-supervised ViT pretraining recipe from Meta (Oquab et al., 2023) that combines a DINO-style self-distillation objective with an iBOT masked-patch prediction objective and a curated 142M-image dataset. DINOv2 produces general-purpose frozen visual features that outperform task-specific supervised baselines on classification, segmentation, depth, and correspondence.
  • SigLIPA contrastive image–text pretraining method (Zhai et al., 2023) that replaces CLIP's softmax-over-batch contrastive loss with a pairwise sigmoid binary cross-entropy. SigLIP removes the need for large global batches, scales batch-size-efficiently, and achieves CLIP-level or better zero-shot accuracy at a fraction of the training compute.
  • Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM)A generative model that learns to reverse a fixed Gaussian corruption process. Ho et al. (2020) showed that predicting the added noise with a neural network, trained by a simple MSE loss on \( T \) diffusion steps, yields state-of-the-art image synthesis — the foundation of all modern image/video diffusion.
  • Classifier-Free GuidanceA sampling-time technique for conditional diffusion models: jointly train a conditional and an unconditional score model, then extrapolate along their difference at inference to amplify the conditioning. Classifier-free guidance replaced classifier-guided diffusion and is the reason text-to-image models follow prompts.
  • Flow Matching / Rectified FlowA generative modelling framework that learns a time-dependent velocity field mapping noise to data along a fixed probability path. Rectified Flow in particular learns straight-line paths between noise and data samples, enabling 1–4 step sampling with quality matching much deeper diffusion models.
  • Latent DiffusionRun the diffusion process in the compressed latent space of a pretrained VAE rather than in pixel space. Latent diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) slashes memory and compute by ~8× for images while preserving sample quality, and is the architecture behind Stable Diffusion, SDXL, SD3, and most text-to-image systems.

  71. Step 71
    Arithmetic Intensity
    Arithmetic intensity \( I = \text{FLOPs}/\text{bytes moved} \) quantifies how much compute a kernel performs per byte it reads or writes. It places any kernel on the roofline: low \( I \) → memory-bound, high \( I \) → compute-bound. Optimisation strategy flips sign with \( I \) — reduce bytes vs reduce FLOPs. Understanding \( I \) is the single most valuable concept for reasoning about GPU performance.

  78. Step 78
    Goodhart's Law and Specification Gaming
    When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. Optimising a proxy metric \( \hat{M} \) for a true-but-unobservable goal \( M \) tends to decouple them once optimisation pressure is strong enough. Specification gaming is the RL-specific manifestation: agents maximise reward by exploiting the reward specification rather than achieving the intended behaviour. These twin failure modes underlie most benchmark-gaming, reward-hacking, and evaluation-contamination stories in modern ML.

  80. Step 80
    GPU Memory Hierarchy and the Roofline Model
    Modern GPUs have a layered memory hierarchy: HBM (high-bandwidth global, ~1–3 TB/s), L2 cache, SRAM / shared memory per SM (~20 TB/s), and registers. The roofline model plots achievable throughput vs arithmetic intensity: low-intensity kernels are memory-bound (limited by HBM bandwidth), high-intensity kernels compute-bound (limited by peak FLOPs). Understanding the roofline is the fastest way to spot the bottleneck of any GPU kernel.

  122. Step 122
    Chinchilla Scaling Laws
    DeepMind's 2022 analysis (Hoffmann et al.) showing that, under a fixed compute budget \( C \approx 6 N D \), optimal pretraining allocates <b>roughly equal compute to parameters and data</b> — meaning parameters \( N \) and training tokens \( D \) should scale in lockstep. This corrected the earlier Kaplan et al. (2020) recommendation to over-grow models and led to the "Chinchilla-optimal" rule-of-thumb \( D \approx 20 N \).

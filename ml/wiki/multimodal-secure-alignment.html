Multimodal Secure Alignment

<||>

The challenge of ensuring safety guardrails hold robustly across fundamentally different data modalities (text, images, audio) when projected into a shared embedding space, preventing cross-modal jailbreaks that bypass text filters.

Illustration

Vision-Language Models (VLMs) encode visual and textual inputs through separate encoders — \( f_{\text{vision}}(I) \) and \( f_{\text{text}}(T) \) — before projecting both into a shared latent space.

The Vulnerability

Safety fine-tuning (RLHF) is overwhelmingly text-centric. The projection layer aligns semantics (a picture of a cat = "cat"), but fails to align safety boundaries. Attackers exploit this via:

  • Typographic Attacks: Rendering textual jailbreaks into PNG images. The vision encoder processes it, entirely bypassing the text safety filter.
  • Modality Switching: Splitting a harmful prompt ("How to synth") in text and ("[picture of meth]") in visual logic. Neither alone crosses the refusal threshold.
  • Adversarial Perturbations: Adding \( \epsilon \)-bounded noise gradient vectors to shift an image's embedding strictly toward harmful distribution regions \( h_{\text{harmful}} \).

Multimodal Secure Alignment requires contrastive training that forces the latent space safety bounds to overlap perfectly across all sensory encoders.

Variational Autoencoder

Step 1 of 5

Motivation & Context

When performing generative modeling, our goal is to model the true data distribution \( p(x) \). Typically, we define a latent variable model \( p(x, z) = p(x|z)p(z) \) where \( z \) represents unobserved factors of variation. To train this model via maximum likelihood, we must marginalize out the latent variables: \( p(x) = \int p(x|z)p(z) dz \).

For any reasonably complex neural network decoder, this integral is intractable. Furthermore, calculating the true posterior \( p(z|x) = p(x|z)p(z)/p(x) \) is universally difficult because it inherently relies on \( p(x) \).

Why not just a standard Autoencoder?

Traditional autoencoders learn a deterministic mapping from inputs to a bottleneck vector \( z \) and back to the reconstructed input. This yields a disjoint, non-smooth latent space. You cannot easily sample an arbitrary \( z \) from a prior and expect the decoder to produce realistic, novel data. A VAE solves this by forcing the encoder to output a continuous distribution over \( z \).

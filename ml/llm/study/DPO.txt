13:T552,<p>By early 2023 the standard recipe for turning a pretrained <a href="/ml/wiki/topics/language-model">language model</a> into an assistant was a three-stage pipeline: <a href="/ml/wiki/topics/supervised-fine-tuning-sft">supervised fine-tuning</a> on demonstrations, training a separate <a href="/ml/wiki/topics/reward-model">reward model</a> on human preference comparisons, and then <a href="/ml/wiki/topics/reinforcement-learning-rl">reinforcement learning</a> against that reward model with <a href="/ml/wiki/topics/proximal-policy-optimization-ppo">PPO</a>. The pipeline — <a href="/ml/wiki/topics/reinforcement-learning-from-human-feedback-rlhf">RLHF</a> — produced InstructGPT, ChatGPT, Claude 1, and every other frontier assistant of the period. It also broke in ways that its users did not enjoy debugging.</p><p>DPO's argument is one sentence long: the reward-model step and the RL step are both unnecessary. A single classification-style loss on preference pairs optimizes the exact same objective that the two-stage RLHF pipeline approximates, without rollouts, without a reward network, and without PPO's hyperparameters. The paper titled its insight &quot;your language model is secretly a reward model&quot; because the mathematical reparametrization that made this work showed the policy itself carries the reward-model signal implicitly.</p>14:T455,<p>The paper's §3 lays out the two-phase objective that DPO replaces.
